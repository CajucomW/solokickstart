Skilled Nursing Facilities 06092019

For many, skilled nursing facilities, or SNFs (pronounced "sniffs"), are viable options for families who have friends and/or loved ones that need medical attention at a daily basis. However, not very many know what to look for when choosing a SNF for a loved one. Here, I'm going to share what I believe makes for a good Skilled Nursing Facility.

  • The Smell

    • Yes, the smell. When you walk into a facility, the first thing you should look for is the smell. It is a great indicator of a well-kept SNF. While 'shopping' around for a SNF take the time to walk halls and pay attention to any foul odors! A well kept SNF will not necessarily smell great, but odor-free is a sign that patients are attended to and that the facilities are clean.

  • The Caseload

    • The size of the facility is important to consider when choosing a SNF. A larger facility need to be staffed properly so the residents aren't spread too thin. In my experience a capable CNA (certified nursing assistant) should be able to attend to a maximum of Five patients at a time. I've found that tending to more than that becomes overwhelming even to capable CNAs.

  • The Staff

    • A brief explanation ofr the teams a SNF typically consist of:

    • Administrative

      • This team coordinates accounts and monetary issues involved during the resident's stay. Any issues with the facility as a whole can be addressed through this team.

    • Nursing

      • The nursing team addresses most things medical-related with the resident. This team ensures medications are administered on time and as prescribed. They also are in charge of the CNAs, who are interact with the residents at a regular basis. This brings me to the next team...

    • CNAs

      • Technically, they are a part of the nursing team. But CNAs roam all over a typical SNF, and commonly are closest to the residents who live there. This group are the ones who interact with the residents during all sorts of daily activities such as dressing, feeding, and toileting. Be nice to the CNAs!

    • Rehabilitation

      • This is my team. The rehab department is responsible for helping the residents recover their previous functional abilities. We help residents improve their balance, strength, coordination, while also increasing their awareness for safety while completing even the most mundane of activities. Our job is to make sure residents do not come back after leaving the SNF by being safer while at home.

There are other teams that make up a SNF like the dietary, maintenance, and social services teams, which are all equally important to consider when deciding on a certain facility. The most important thing to consider is that all teams are on the same page in regards to your loved one's needs.
